Astronomy Olympiad Notes.

Introduction

During my time in high school, I participated in the United States Astronomy and Astrophysics Olympiad (USAAAO) for three years, as well as the International Olympiad in Astronomy and Astrophysics for the last two of those years, winning gold medals in both years. In my senior year of high school, I also helped contribute problems to the USAAAO.

Comments on Astro Problem Writing

In my senior year of high school, I decided to try out something newâ€”instead of competing for a fourth consecutive year, I helped write questions for the USAAAO organization. I ended up contributing L1 (Long Question #1) on the 2022 NAC (see here), which was the problem about the James Webb Space Telescope. As a side note, I’m proud that I wrote a problem about the JWST before the 2022 IPhO (and likely the 2022 IOAA), which featured problems about the JWST as well.

When I wrote my problem, I intended it to be a unique, unprecedented problem, but one that was solvable given standard knowledge of astrophysics and some mathematical prowess in approximations. I wanted the problem’s subparts to contain a range of difficulty, with the most unique subpart (part (d)) being worth the most points. I did not expect the problem to be so difficult amongst the competitors; even within the eventual top scorers, most of the competitors obtained 10 points or fewer out of a possible 45. The top scorer on this question only obtained around 30 points. If you took the 2022 NAC, on behalf of the USAAAO problem writing committee, I’m sorry about problem L1. You probably should’ve started with problem L2 before doing problem L1.

Ironically, my problem is not covered in any of my study notes linked above. That’s because my study notes are not representative of all the material that I studied for astronomy and astrophysics olympiads; they’re only a concise summary of the most common topics, plus some extra handouts on several topics I found to be confusing. However, it should have been the warning sign to me that my problem may not have been accessible to most astronomy olympiad competitors.
